Treatment and Help

The NHS offers low-intensity treatments, talking therapy and medication to treat anxiety disorders. Some people will need both at the same time. Low-intensity treatments include: Non-facilitated self-help, which means that you will try to help yourself using information from the NHS.

Guided self-help, supported by a trained professional, who delivers the self-help programme and reviews progress and outcomes and, 5 to 7 weekly or fornightly face-to-face or telephone sessions. Also available is Psycho educational groups, which means that you will learn about your symptoms and how to manage them. Including presentations from a trained professional, self-help manuals, and based on CBT.

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) helps you undrstand the links between your thoughts, feelings and behaviour. It can help you to find ways to overcome your anxiety by challenging negative thoughts and beliefs. Another treatment is Applied Relaxation, which means that you will focus on relaxing your muscles in a certain way. And at a certain time. For example learning how you can relax your muscles so that you are able to fall asleep easier.

Exposure and response prevention (ERP) is effective for a range of anxiety disorders, particularly obsessive compulsive disorder (OCD). Your therapist will encourage you to experience your obsessive thoughts and help you to manage them in a different way. They will build up the difficulty of each task.
